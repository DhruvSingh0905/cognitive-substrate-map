A Network-Control Approach to a
Learning-Optimal Brain State

Dhruv Singh

Independent project · July 2026 · code at github.com/DhruvSingh0905/cognitive-substrate-map

Abstract. We cast a biomedical knowledge graph as a signed, directed control system and ask a control-theoretic question of it: which genes should be perturbed, and by how much, to steer the brain toward a learning-optimal state at minimal systemic cost? Starting from PrimeKG[1] (129,375 nodes), we scope to a 280-node brain core (2,871 signed edges, a 200-node feedback component), propagate perturbations by random-walk-with-restart / Personalized PageRank[4], analyze structural controllability[9], define a set-point-aware objective, quantify uncertainty by Monte-Carlo propagation[12] with Sobol attribution[13], and trace the benefit–cost Pareto front by the ε-constraint method[15]. The engine is validated by sign-consistency against curated regulatory edges (8/8). The deliverable is a ranking of interventions and a trade-off map — not fold-change predictions, and not a protocol.

Keywords: knowledge graphs · network propagation · structural controllability · multi-objective optimization · computational neuroscience

1. Substrate

The base graph is PrimeKG[1] ($|V|=129{,}375$, $|E|\approx4.05\times10^{6}$, ten node types, thirty edge types), whose lineage is the Hetionet / Rephetio hetnet built to repurpose drugs by scoring metapaths from compound to disease[2].

Brain-scoping

A gene $v$ is retained by a tissue-expression enrichment score over PrimeKG's anatomy_protein_present edges $E_a$:

$$e(v)=\frac{\bigl|\{a:(v,a)\in E_a,\ a\in A_{\mathrm{brain}}\}\bigr|}{\bigl|\{a:(v,a)\in E_a\}\bigr|},\qquad \text{keep if } e(v)\ge 0.15 \ \wedge\ \deg_a(v)\ge 5 \tag{1}$$

In words — keep a gene only if a meaningful fraction of the tissues it is expressed in are brain tissues, and only if it is expressed in enough places for that fraction to be trustworthy.

After pruning, the substrate has $N=280$ nodes in four layers (intervention 70, input 15, readout 6, trap 2, bounded expansion 187), $3{,}912$ directed edges of which $2{,}871$ are signed ($+{:}\,2253,\ -{:}\,618$), and a single strongly-connected component of $200$ nodes.

2. The propagation operator

Let $W\in\{-1,0,+1\}^{N\times N}$ be the signed adjacency, oriented so that $(Wx)_i$ is the influx to node $i$: $W_{ij}=\operatorname{sign}(j\to i)$. With out-strength $d^{\mathrm{out}}_j=\sum_i|W_{ij}|$ and $D_{\mathrm{out}}=\operatorname{diag}(d^{\mathrm{out}})$, we column-normalize into the Personalized-PageRank transition operator[3]:

$$\hat{W}=W\,D_{\mathrm{out}}^{-1},\qquad \hat{W}_{ij}=\frac{W_{ij}}{\sum_k|W_{kj}|} \tag{2}$$

In words — split each gene's outgoing influence evenly across the targets it regulates, so a "hub" gene that talks to everyone cannot dominate the network.

Because $|\hat{W}|$ is column-stochastic, Perron–Frobenius gives $\rho(\hat{W})\le\rho(|\hat{W}|)=1$, which guarantees the propagation of §3 converges (measured: $\rho(\hat W)=0.544$). The column form is the correct choice for a directed regulatory graph, where promiscuity is an out-degree property; the symmetric GCN normalization $D^{-1/2}\tilde{W}D^{-1/2}$[6] is the undirected analogue.

3. Propagation by random-walk-with-restart

Inject a perturbation $p\in\mathbb{R}^N$ (push one or more genes) and diffuse it with restart probability $\alpha$ — the random-walk-with-restart / APPNP recurrence[5],[4]:

$$x^{(t+1)}=(1-\alpha)\,\hat{W}x^{(t)}+\alpha\,p \tag{3}$$

In words — at every step, spread the signal one hop through the network, but yank a fixed fraction $\alpha$ of it back to the gene you started from.

The fixed point solves $(I-(1-\alpha)\hat{W})x^{*}=\alpha p$. Since $\rho((1-\alpha)\hat{W})\le 1-\alpha<1$, the inverse exists and equals its Neumann series:

$$x^{*}=\alpha\bigl(I-(1-\alpha)\hat{W}\bigr)^{-1}p=\alpha\sum_{k\ge0}(1-\alpha)^{k}\hat{W}^{k}p \tag{4}$$

In words — the settled-down response. Equivalently, add up the signal arriving over every path length $k$, each discounted by $(1-\alpha)^k$ — so distant hops fade exponentially.

Two consequences: the mean influence radius is $\bar k=(1-\alpha)/\alpha\approx5.7$ hops at $\alpha=0.15$; and as $\alpha\to0$, $x^{*}$ collapses to the leading eigenvector of $\hat{W}$ (source-independent — the over-smoothing failure mode), which the $(1-\alpha)^k$ decay prevents. The 200-node feedback component is why a steady-state resolvent, not a one-shot push, is required.

4. Controllability

Linearizing the regulatory dynamics as an LTI system with input map $B\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times m}$ (the nodes we can drive), $\dot{x}=Wx+Bu$, the pair $(W,B)$ is controllable iff the controllability matrix has full rank (Kalman[7]):

$$\operatorname{rank}\mathcal{C}=N,\qquad \mathcal{C}=[\,B,\ WB,\ W^{2}B,\ \dots,\ W^{N-1}B\,] \tag{5}$$

In words — you can drive the system to any state exactly when your control inputs, pushed through successive network hops, together reach every one of the $N$ dimensions.

Testing rank directly is impractical at scale, so we use structural controllability (Lin[8]; Liu–Slotine–Barabási[9]): the minimum number of driver nodes equals the count left unmatched by a maximum matching $M^{*}$ on the bipartite representation of the graph,

$$N_{D}=\max\bigl(N-|M^{*}|,\ 1\bigr) \tag{6}$$

In words — the fewest genes you must directly control is the number of nodes left over after pairing up the network as efficiently as possible.

The matching is computed by Hopcroft–Karp[10] in $O(|E|\sqrt{N})$; denser wiring yields a larger matching and hence fewer drivers, $\langle k\rangle\propto1/N_D$. On the substrate: all 280 nodes are reachable from the druggable set, full control would require $N_D=81$ drivers (only 35 druggable) — so arbitrary-state control fails, but target control[11] of the readout subset needs $\le N_D$ inputs and is feasible, which is the regime the optimizer uses.

5. Target state and objective

The target is a deviation vector $d\in\mathbb{R}^N$. Most cognitive variables are not "maximize" but set-points with an inverted-U response (Yerkes–Dodson[18]; Arnsten[19]): dopamine, arousal, cortisol, excitation/inhibition balance, mTOR. Benefit is the reduction in distance to the target:

$$b_i=\lvert d_i\rvert-\lvert x^{*}_i-d_i\rvert \tag{7}$$

In words — how much closer to the ideal level this gene got: positive if the perturbation moved it toward the target, negative if it overshot past it.

Aggregate benefit weights each node by confidence $w_i$ (readouts get $w_i=0$); cost prices intervention effort (an $L_1$ term rewarding few knobs) plus off-target collateral:

$$B(p)=\sum_i w_i\,b_i,\qquad C(p)=\lVert p\rVert_1+\gamma\!\!\sum_{j\,\notin\,T}\!\!\lvert x^{*}_j\rvert \tag{8}$$

In words — total good across all genes, against total cost: how hard you had to push, plus how much you disturbed things outside the target set (a side-effect proxy).

The curated vector covers 94 genes: 43 set-point, 30 up, 12 down.

6. Uncertainty quantification

Many edge magnitudes are sign-only and some targets are low-confidence, so a point estimate would overstate precision. We propagate the input uncertainty $\theta$ by Monte-Carlo (the GUM-S1 procedure[12]): draw $\theta^{(m)}$, evaluate $Y^{(m)}=B(p;\theta^{(m)})$, and report an order-statistic coverage interval:

$$\widehat{\mathrm{CI}}_{90\%}=\bigl[\,Y_{(\lceil0.05M\rceil)},\ Y_{(\lfloor0.95M\rfloor)}\,\bigr] \tag{9}$$

In words — run the model many times under the assumed uncertainty, sort the resulting scores, and report the middle 90% as an honest range rather than a single number.

We attribute the band with Sobol sensitivity indices[13],[14] (first-order and total-effect):

$$S_i=\frac{\operatorname{Var}_{\theta_i}\!\bigl(\mathbb{E}[Y\mid\theta_i]\bigr)}{\operatorname{Var}(Y)}, \qquad S_{T_i}=\frac{\mathbb{E}\bigl[\operatorname{Var}(Y\mid\theta_{\sim i})\bigr]}{\operatorname{Var}(Y)} \tag{10}$$

In words — of all the wobble in the final score, how much is each uncertain input responsible for — so you know which single fact to go verify to shrink the range the most.

7. Optimization

The problem is genuinely multi-objective — maximize $B$, minimize $C$ — so the honest solution is a Pareto front, traced exactly by the ε-constraint method[15]:

$$\max_p\ B(p)\quad\text{s.t.}\quad C(p)\le\varepsilon,\qquad \varepsilon\in[\varepsilon_{\min},\varepsilon_{\max}] \tag{11}$$

In words — get the most benefit you can while keeping cost under a cap $\varepsilon$, then slide the cap from tight to loose to draw out the whole benefit-vs-cost trade-off curve.

A weighted-sum scalarization $\max_p B(p)-\lambda C(p)$ is a supporting hyperplane of normal $(1,\lambda)$: it recovers only points on the convex hull of the front, so on a non-convex front it provably misses concave regions for every $\lambda$[16],[17]. The ε-constraint method does not, and the small state space makes the exact inner solve affordable.

8. Validation

The engine is test-driven (11 tests): the asserted invariants include $\rho(\hat W)\le1$, agreement between the resolvent (4) and its truncated series to $10^{-8}$, and sign propagation on hand-built graphs. The load-bearing check compares $\operatorname{sign}(x^{*})$ against edge signs read directly from the database — not the model ($\alpha=0.15$, $\rho(\hat W)=0.544$).

9. Groundedness and assumptions

The layers sit on different evidential rungs: brain-scoping is computed; edge sign and direction are curated from causal databases; edge magnitude is mostly sign-only; the objective consumes a hand-built target vector. Crucially, $\hat{W}$ is the first-order Jacobian of the true nonlinear dynamics at baseline, so $x^{*}$ is valid only for modest $\lVert x-x_0\rVert$; large perturbations leave the trust region (saturation, sign flips). The deliverable is therefore an ordinal ranking of interventions and a trade-off map — robust to the linearization — not fold-change magnitudes, and not medical advice.
